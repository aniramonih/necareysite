Early Career Highlights

Freddy Kempf made his mark on the classical music world at a young age. His early accolades include winning the first National Mozart Competition in England in 1987 and being named the BBC Young Musician of the Year in 1992 for his rendition of Rachmaninoff’s Rhapsody on a Theme of Paganini.

These achievements highlighted his burgeoning talent and paved the way for his international career.

Debut and Recognition

In 1996, Kempf’s career took a significant leap forward when he won the Young Concert Artists International Auditions, which led to his recital debut at Carnegie Hall in New York City. This debut was a critical stepping stone, establishing him as a serious contender in the classical music scene.

Later Career Development

Kempf’s career trajectory experienced a notable moment at the 1998 International Tchaikovsky Competition in Moscow. Although he did not win, the competition sparked controversy and brought him considerable attention.

He secured the third prize, but his performance was so compelling that it led to a surge in his popularity in Russia, reminiscent of the acclaim received by American pianist Van Cliburn in 1958.

Ongoing Contributions and Performances

Throughout his career, Kempf has continued to perform extensively across Europe, the Americas, East Asia, and Australia. His repertoire includes works by Bach, Beethoven, Chopin, Liszt, Prokofiev, Rachmaninoff, and Schumann.

In 2001, his contributions to music were further recognized when he was voted Best Young British Classical Performer at the Classical BRIT Awards.

Chamber Music and The Kempf Trio

In 2000, Kempf founded the Kempf Trio with Pierre Bensaid (violin) and Alexander Chaushian (cello). The trio has gained acclaim for their performances in Europe and internationally, including their U.S. debut in the chamber music series at La Jolla, California, and Scottsdale, Arizona.
